James Webb Telescope

If you’re a science freak then make no mistake in not reading this article about the “JAMES WEBB TELESCOPE” because believe me it’s a beautiful piece of astro-technical equipment that’s gonna tantalize you.

The JWST or the “WEBB” is a space telescope that is planned to be the successor of the “Hubble Space Telescope”. The HST was launched on April 24 1990 and has done its part in space exploration as one of the most versatile and powerful telescope to be ever created by human kind. It paved way for solving the mysteries of the universe such as data on exo-planets, pinning down the age of the universe, discovery of galaxies in the early age of formation of universe and many more. Sadly, everything has an expiry date and the HST has proven its might. Its time for the JWST to do its work.

The JWST is set to launch in March 2021 despite delays and other technical issues. It has an advantage over the Hubble as it provide a higher infrared resolution and sensitivity which enables a wide range of investigation across the field of astronomy and cosmology including formation of stars and planets, imaging of exo-planets and nova.

The primary mirror of the JWST, the Optical Telescope Element, is composed of 18 hexagonal mirror segments made of gold-plated beryllium which combine to create a 6.5-meter (21 ft; 260 in) diameter mirror that is much larger than the Hubble's 2.4-meter (7.9 ft; 94 in) mirror. Unlike the Hubble, which observes in the near ultraviolet, visible, and near infrared (0.1 to 1 μm) spectra, the JWST will observe in a lower frequency range, from long-wavelength visible light through mid-infrared (0.6 to 28.3 μm), which will allow it to observe high redshift objects that are too old and too distant for the Hubble to observe.[10][11] The telescope must be kept very cold in order to observe in the infrared without interference, so it will be deployed in space near the Earth–Sun L2 Lagrangian point, and a large sunshield made of silicon- and aluminium-coated Kapton will keep its mirror and instruments below 50 K (−220 °C; −370 °F). It has a NIRCam, NIRSpec, MIRI, FGS/NIRISS guiders, sensors and spectrometers on it to provide most authentic observation and stability.

The JWST is being developed by NASA—with significant contributions from the European Space Agency and the Canadian Space Agency[1]—and is named for James E. Webb, who was the administrator of NASA from 1961 to 1968 and played an integral role in the Apollo program.

This one-of-a-kind telescope which takes space exploration to the next dimension to reveal its mysteries beyond reality is set to launch soon. So, stay tuned.
